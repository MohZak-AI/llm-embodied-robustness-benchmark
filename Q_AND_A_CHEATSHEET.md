# Presentation Q&A Cheat Sheet
**Potential questions from professors or audience members during the seminar, and how to scientifically answer them.**

---

### Q1: "If these are Vision-Language Models (VLMs), why didn't you just test them with actual images? Doesn't using text defeat the purpose?"
**Your Answer:**
*"That’s a great question, but using images actually creates a massive testing loophole. If we showed a picture of a spatial layout to the AI and it got the answer wrong, we wouldn't know exactly 'where' inside the neural network the break happened. Did the AI's 'Vision Encoder' (its eyes) glitch out because of pixel blurring? Or did its 'Reasoning Engine' (its brain) fail the logic? By exclusively using 100% loss-less text proxies, we completely bypassed the eyes. We scientifically proved that the core reasoning brain itself is fundamentally flawed, without the distraction of vision errors."*

### Q2: "You mentioned the 'Cow in the Microwave' test. It's funny, but how does an absurd test like that actually matter for real-world AI deployment?"
**Your Answer:**
*"We don't actually care about cows in microwaves. We care about the mechanism it exposes: Hallucination in the face of anomaly. If an AI refuses to believe a text prompt because its 'commonsense training bias' says it's impossible, that means it will also ignore reality in critical situations. For example, if a medical AI sees an X-ray of organs on the wrong side of the body, or a self-driving car sees an airplane crashed on a highway, we need to mathematically guarantee the AI acts on 'what is actually happening in reality,' rather than 'what it statistically thinks should be there.' Tests like ROME verify that safety threshold."*

### Q3: "You showed a 'mini' model beating giant models like GPT-4o. How is that possible? Isn't bigger always better in AI?"
**Your Answer:**
*"That’s the exact paradigm shift currently happening in AI. Coding or writing essays requires huge 'knowledge vaults,' so having billions of parameters (a massive model) makes sense. But spatial logic puzzles don't require encyclopedic memory; they require strict rule-following and focus. A model like `gpt-5-mini` uses 'inference compute'—it is heavily trained to pause, read constraints, and suppress its fast, generalized web-based reflexes. It trades sheer memorization bulk for disciplined logic tracing, which perfectly suits Embodied AI."*

### Q4: "You highlighted 'Prompt Sensitivity' as a failure. But isn't it obvious that if you word things confusingly, a robot might mess up?"
**Your Answer:**
*"It shouldn't be for an intelligent agent! If I tell a human, 'The apple is to the left of the banana,' or 'Left of the banana sits the apple,' the physical 3D scene in the human's mind doesn't change. However, when we swapped syntax for the AI, their pass rates plummeted to 52%. This is terrifying because it proves the models aren't actually mapping a 3D structural understanding of the world; they are just doing shallow, pattern-matched linguistic guessing. If we rely on robotic automation, their logic cannot shatter just because a human issued a command using slightly different syntax."*

### Q5: "What is the hardest problem to solve based on your benchmark?" 
**Your Answer:**
*"Topic 3: Safety and Autonomy (dynamic action sequencing). It is far easier for an AI to act as a 'passive observer' and statically identify objects on a table. But the moment you ask an AI to sequence physical actions around safety boundaries (like cleaning a desk without throwing away a laptop), its logic degrades sharply. Moving from 'Observation' to 'Dynamic Action' is the current ceiling for embodied agents."*
